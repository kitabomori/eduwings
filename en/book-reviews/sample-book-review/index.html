Review: Educated by Tara Westover

10 June 2026

A critical review of Tara Westover's memoir and its relevance to education and self-determination.

Title: Educated Author: Tara Westover Publisher: Random House Year: 2018

Tara Westover’s memoir is the story of a girl who had never attended school until she was seventeen and who eventually earned a doctorate from Cambridge University. It is not simply a success story — it is an account of the tension between education and family, knowledge and loyalty, self-discovery and the cost of leaving behind the world you were born into.

Summary: Westover grew up in a survivalist family in rural Idaho, cut off from formal schooling, medical care, and many of the shared assumptions of mainstream American life. Her path into education was self-directed, painful, and transformative. The book traces that path with extraordinary precision and honesty.

Strengths: The writing is lucid and often beautiful. Westover does not romanticise either her upbringing or her eventual escape from it. The book raises genuinely difficult questions about what it means to be educated — whether education is a credential, a practice of self-knowledge, or something that can be achieved without institutions at all.

Limitations: The memoir is, by nature, a single perspective. Readers should approach some of its more dramatic claims about Westover’s family with awareness of this limitation.

Relevance to education: For researchers and practitioners working on access, equity, and the social function of schooling, this book is essential reading. It is particularly valuable for those working in contexts where education is not yet universal — including Pakistan — because it shows, without sentimentality, what formal education makes possible and what it costs.

Recommendation: Highly recommended for educators, researchers, and anyone who has ever wondered whether school systems serve the people they claim to serve.
