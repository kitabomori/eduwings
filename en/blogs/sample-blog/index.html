Why Reading Aloud Still Matters in Pakistani Classrooms

20 June 2026

Despite digital pressures and curriculum overload, the practice of reading aloud remains one of the most powerful tools a teacher has.

In most government schools across Pakistan, the method of teaching follows a single script: the teacher speaks, the student writes. The textbook is an instrument, not an invitation. This is not a new problem, but its consequences are becoming harder to ignore.

Research consistently shows that when children read aloud regularly — whether listening to a teacher’s voice or using their own — their vocabulary, comprehension, and reading fluency improve significantly. Yet in our classrooms, this practice is disappearing.

Reversing this trend does not require a new budget or a policy overhaul. It requires a teacher willing to spend five minutes every day reading from a book out loud.

The habit of listening to a story, following words on a page, and associating reading with pleasure rather than examination pressure is one of the earliest and most durable gifts an educator can give. It is also one of the cheapest. That it is so rarely given tells us something important about what we believe education is for.
